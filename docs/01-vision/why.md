# Why We're Building Binding Chaos

## The Problem

Most tools for community decision-making were built around the same assumption: someone needs to be in charge. A moderator approves posts. A committee reviews proposals. A vote decides the outcome. Even well-intentioned platforms tend to recreate hierarchy because that's the shape their underlying model assumes.

This creates familiar failure modes. Decisions bottleneck at whoever holds authority. Binary votes flatten complex issues into yes/no. Formal processes exclude people who don't have time or confidence to navigate them. And when trust in the central authority erodes — as it usually does eventually — the whole system wobbles.

There's a deeper problem too. Most platforms optimize for engagement rather than progress. Content that provokes reaction rises to the top. Important but quiet problems stay invisible. The community's attention gets captured rather than directed.

## What We're Trying Instead

The core idea is simple: **when the work is visible, people can find where they're needed.**

Instead of asking who should decide, we ask: what do people in this community actually notice? What ideas does that give rise to? What's being worked on? What has changed in the world as a result?

If that information is legible to everyone, coordination can happen without anyone directing it. People follow the traces others leave — the way ants follow pheromone trails, or contributors find open issues on a well-maintained project. This is what Heather Marsh calls stigmergic coordination: emergent, bottom-up collaboration guided by the environment rather than by instruction.

Visibility, in this model, isn't just a feature. It's the accountability mechanism. There's no need for a committee to evaluate whether something matters — if it matters, people engage with it. There's no need for an authority to declare success — when the river gets cleaned up, everyone can see it.

## What We're Building

Binding Chaos is a platform where communities can:

- **Capture signals** — name problems, needs, or observations from the real world
- **Develop ideas** — propose, discuss, amend, fork, and build on responses to those signals
- **Take action** — coordinate around ideas that gain traction, in whatever form that takes
- **See what changes** — connect real-world outcomes back to the signals that prompted them

The signal stays open until something in the world actually changes. That's the unit of accountability. Not a vote passed, not a proposal approved — a real-world difference that the community can point to.

## What We're Not Building

We're not building a voting system. Votes create winners and losers, compress nuance, and tend to hand power to whoever controls the ballot.

We're not building a forum. Forums are good for conversation but poor at connecting discussion to action or tracking what actually happened as a result.

We're not building a project management tool. The coordination here is emergent, not assigned.

## Inspiration

This project is directly inspired by Heather Marsh's *Binding Chaos: Mass Collaboration on a Global Scale*. Marsh argues that the problems facing communities — local and global — are too complex and too interconnected for hierarchical systems to solve. What's needed instead is mass collaboration that can operate without central control: transparent, accountable, and driven by the people closest to the problems.

We're trying to build the infrastructure that makes that possible.

## What We Don't Know Yet

This is an honest document, so it should include this: we're not sure how well stigmergic coordination works in practice at the scales we're imagining. The theory is compelling. The examples — open source software, Wikipedia, certain social movements — are encouraging. But a purpose-built platform for civic and community coordination is uncharted territory.

We're building it incrementally. We'll surface content by recent activity to start, and change the approach if that turns out to suppress what matters. We'll see what "signal addressed" looks like in practice before we over-engineer the resolution mechanism. We'll let the system's own traces tell us what's working.
